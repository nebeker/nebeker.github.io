The Organic and Social Nature of Software

  • Communication
  • Software
  • UX

Published on 2026-05-25

Programming has changed a lot through the years. Over the last few decades it seems like programmers have been competing over who uses the most arcane and inscrutable technology and process - see xkcd Real Programmers for reference. These days there’s a cultural shift to prove either we can be “orchestrators” or “supervisors” of generative Artificial Intelligence agents or we’re unnecessary and solo founders can “vibe code” their entire business. These extremes are predicated on the assumption that a computer program is a collection of lines of code.

I’m a software engineer working in industrial automation and in this article I will argue that a program or digital system is not its source code or binaries, but the constellation of client and user requirements shaped by business and technical constraints, as it exists in the minds of the people responsible for it. I’ll support this idea with key bibliography as well as by recounting a few personal experiences in software project onboarding.

Programming as Theory Building

Please read the paper Programming as Theory Building by Peter Naur. I’m sure you can get to the full text. You don’t have to come back to this blog, it’s fine. I intend to add more to it, but if I can get you to read those few pages, I’ll be happy.

Things have changed since 1985, when this paper was published, but the fundamental context of enterprise software remains the same: we build systems that drive business, we have complex requirements and lofty goals and we expect to get our money’s worth out of them, by keeping them in service as long as the business goals remain, with consistency and resiliency.

Naur examines case studies of software system lifecycles and concludes that the code, the specification and the documentation attached to them is, inherently, incomplete and not representative of the program. Over handovers to different teams and the disparate mental models of the system held by its original engineers, the service teams and the new teams, the author notes that the program can die and be born anew, with direct business impacts, as the separate parties act on their own understanding.

I’ll go into my own case studies later, but I can already share that this deeply resonates with my own experience in this business. As the author points out, I too have made implicit assumptions and architecture or implementation decisions I can scarcely explain. No, I will not be expanding on my years of experience in industrial automation and manufacturing in each Jira ticket I prepare for an engineer. Yes, I expect they’ll act on their own experience and encourage them to ask question and even to teach me about the things their alternate path has allowed them to learn. These conversations will lead to a shared understanding of the system that can’t fully be written down.

This is the core of my argument, but I’ll add further points supported by other works.

The Design of Everyday Things

Continuing with the reading list, The Design of Everyday Things by Donald Norman was originally titled The Psychology of Everyday Things. I only learned this piece of trivia while writing this article, but it matches what I noticed reading the 2013 Revised and Expanded Edition. The author walks us through years of experience, case studies, mental exercises and discussions to reveal how system design lives and dies by the psychology of designers and users.

A device, a system, a thing one can interact with is perceived and an idea of it can be built in our minds. Norman uses the perceptual psychology term “affordance” to describe the features that indicate a possible action: a door knob can be turned to open the door, a button can be clicked with a mouse or tapped with a finger to execute an action. Noticing affordances is the first step in building a mental model. Each person will have a different mental model and its the designer’s aim is to provide mappings between the interaction surfaces and the system’s functionality that make it easier to achieve the desired effects. The most famous example of this is the so-called “Norman door,” a door which presents affordances which make it hard to use, like having bars on both sides. The mapping between interaction surfaces and functionality is unclear: should one just push on the bar? Or maybe pull on the bar? Perhaps lift the door off its hinges?

I’ve written a bit about the effects of setting up poor mapping for mental models in an earlier blog post. Another concept from The Design of Everyday Things that comes into play in software systems is that of knowledge in the head vs knowledge in the world: a manual is knowledge in the world, while experience in the field creates knowledge in the head. An uncheck checkbox for “I have read and accept the Privacy Policy” before a disabled “Proceed to payment” button puts the knowledge that accepting the policy is required to make a purchase into the world.

In software we work extensively with different mental models and with knowledge in the head. As above, I assume a certain amount of knowledge about my field is in the heads of my colleagues and won’t be putting it into the world every time. It may or may not be written down somewhere already, perhaps in docs for dependencies or in the codebases of other projects or it might have been learned through observation. I will, however, write down and explain what is specific and not obvious to me. That last bit is important, I may over or underestimate the singularity of some point and I may miss something entirely. That said, a process in code may be that knowledge in the world, if it makes a critical path unambiguous and discoverable. Here collaboration is key, so somebody reading a spec, a piece of documentation or code can ask “why is this here” or “why didn’t you do X?”

The mental model of a system depends on the people interacting with it. It’s not uniform and it’s very complex or very simple depending on what each person can operate or has in the past. The developer leading a feature will view it differently from one working on something else, the customer service engineers supporting it will have a different perspective and the users a different one still. It’s not possible or desirable to have it any other way: the user doesn’t need to concern themselves with the database schema behind a feature, they need good data design that makes their processes clear and smooth. The service engineer should have some knowledge of the deeper backend works, to provide effective support, and may even be a crucial bridge to the developer who needs to implement a bug fix. The program exists in all of these people’s heads.

Clean Code

My final reading suggestion to discuss this topic is the influential book Clean Code: A Handbook of Agile Software Craftsmanship by Robert C. Martin, affectionately known as Uncle Bob. It motivates a shift from understanding code as not only functional and made for a purpose, but as something to be re-read, maintained and expanded upon. It offers strategies to write code that survives this journey.

I only read this a couple of years ago, with some enterprise experience under my belt, and it felt, at times, like common sense or just a good explanation of best practices I’d already picked up. In fact, the book comes from a collection of best practices Uncle Bob picked up over the years working on enterprise software. It was also, as above, very influential, so by the time I got to it many of its innovations had become mostly standard practice.

Clean Code does a very good job of turning a lot of gut feelings into clear written rules, mostly obviously in the long list of code smells. Conversations along the lines of “this doesn’t feel right” can benefit from this body of knowledge and arguments. It’s not common sense or a gut feeling if you can back it up. Having these heuristics spread through the developer community and take root in people’s brains also makes for a shared understanding that allows us to skip documenting some of our decisions. That, in turn, puts more of our systems into our brains.

An important code smells is framed as “comments lie.” Maybe not when they’re written, but likely once the code around them gets refactored, restructured, or is even left dangling. I once dealt with a super convenient database-side enum definition that happened to be super outdated. That was a fun one.

The book also puts a lot of emphasis on test-driven development. This is the practice of writing tests for a feature before writing the code that executes it. The test outlines the intent and the expectations so that new code should only be accepted once it works as it should.

Test-driven development, as a practice, has been widely explored within the software community, gaining advocates and detractors, but Clean Code explores a facet of it that gets less attention than the development process itself: exploring an existing system through tests. Martin explains that a developer can verify their understanding of a program by writing tests that match their mental model. These may pass, confirming and validating the assumptions they’re built on, or fail, leading to a path of deeper analysis that will surface greater insight about the system. This is presented as a potential approach to familiarize oneself with a legacy project.

By binging common principles, concepts, and gut feelings into the developer community’s shared consciousness, the author allows us to move forward in a kind of direction that should work, in most cases, skipping over the cognitive and procedural load of reinventing and communicating these best practices. The process of testing before, during and after development also allows us to put more of our programs’ assumptions into code, if and when we need to, including after the original team has left. The fact that they’ll fail if they’re not kept up to date makes them far more useful than mere comments.

Gang of Four Design Patterns

The earlier Design Patterns: Elements of Reusable Object-Oriented Software by the “Gang of Four” - Erich Gamma, Richard Helm, Ralph Johnson, John Vlissides - discusses object-oriented programming and describes a set of design patterns. These are common approaches to common problems, implemented by many standard libraries, providing a good baseline for many systems. This isn’t strictly a reading recommendation, since I haven’t read the book myself, I’ve just studied many of the patterns it explains.

This foundational work allows engineers to make reasonably safe and appropriate decisions in their work that often require little explanation or justification. The choice to use a particular design patter is based on the engineer’s understanding of and preferred balance between business requirements. It may seem self-evident and it may allow others to gleam insight into what the original constraints might have been, upon reading the implementation in code. It could lead a new team to the wrong conclusions, if the decision was made to satisfy specific constraints that are no longer known or even valid.

When I encounter evidence of a technical choice that seems outdated and mismatched to the system’s current state, I tend to say it’s there for “historical reasons” like a medieval arch one might find in a now-modern European city. Arches are very stable and transfer weight very effectively. They can be built of hand-cut stone and last centuries. In sum, arches are a tried-and-tested design pattern. I see them in bridges very often, but now I tend to see cable-stayed bridges more often. Is that a better design pattern? Did the other bridges use the wrong one? Old construction was subject to different constraints and we should not infer the full set of constraints based on the design choice that resulted from limitations in materials and infrastructure.

Common design patterns help us make decisions and communicate, but don’t tell the full story of a system. A developer coming into a legacy project or a service engineer handling a ticket can make inferences based on these, but may not have the full picture.

My Case Studies

Client A - Handing Over a Baby

A few years ago I was assigned a “small three to four week work package” for a project. These have been the longest three to four weeks of my life. This is a challenging project that’s much bigger in scope than what I’d worked on before. The relationship with the client was strained from a long series of communication issues and other factors, and it was a pain point for the teams around me responsible for supporting it.

When I joined this team the object was that small work package, but I dove in head first into bug fixing and incremental improvements. Some of the things I’m most proud of implementing were User Experience improvements that revealed more and better information about what the system was doing. I also worked to resolve a large number of localization issues that made for a worse UX. Ultimately I’m now a subject matter expert and architect for this project, with the original engineers having moved on, and my responsibility in no longer in implementation but in guidance.

This journey was possible because an original engineer was consistently available to the new team while we worked on our first features. Not full time, but enough that we were able to sync almost daily and learn the ropes from the people who set them up. I had many opportunities to ask one of my favorite types of question: “maybe I’m missing something, but I think this means that…” Sometimes I had my mental model verified, other times I had it refined. As we continued to update the system to serve evolving business needs and as we had our own impact on different components, more and more of the program moved from the original engineers’ brains and into the new team’s. Gradually the conversations started changing. I asked fewer questions and made more assertions. Those assertions started to be validated with “that makes sense, you guys know more about this project than me at this point.”

We also worked closely with our service colleagues giving them access to the engineers’ mental model of the system they hadn’t had before. We were able to explain the reasoning behind the differences between the database schema and the frontend views. We collaborated in quality assurance testing before delivering new features, which confronted the developers with usage patterns that were closer to real-world scenarios than to the specifications. By engaging with all parties involved we measurably increased customer satisfaction.

There was one point where a task with a cleanly defined business scope, but significant technical impact became inescapable. A different developer was brought in with instructions to not interfere with the core team’s work. I spent a few hours here and there providing guidance, but was expected to continue to focus almost exclusively on my own work. This was a stressful time for everyone involved, but it also taught us all valuable lessons. This engineer was constrained in how to get enough context about the system. The output was poor in quality and required extensive review and rework by the core team. I’m not assigning blame, but I could almost hear Peter Naur saying “I told you so” in my ear throughout that process.

This project demonstrated to me the value in a gradual handover and in a social and collaborative approach to a system that acknowledges and leverages different scopes of knowledge and different mental models, over a quick introduction followed by isolated work.

Client B - A Phoenix

An old system needed a new feature. Very simple. The issue is the old system had a purpose my team was not familiar with, despite being ready for the new feature we were already used to. Without getting into details, this was roughly a different sub-business in our industry.

How do you approach this? As we established, you collect knowledge about the system from the people who worked on it before. We only had access to a few hours a week from the former architect, due to perfectly reasonable organizational limitations. That means doing as much legwork as possible to ask the right questions, really get as much juice out of those meetings as possible. Because of the significant difference in scope, the new team was saddled with a set of unknown unkowns - not just things we didn’t know, but things we had no idea to look for.

Without hands-on time on the project with the previous architect, we had to go with different strategies. We had access to the former project manager, so we were able to get more functional information, but were still essentially talking about a different field, so the differences in mental models were complex in non-obvious ways.

OK, next option? A two-pronged approach: on one hand, we started work on our new feature in the most isolated way we could; on the other hand, to prepare the integration, we read a lot of code and we worked on tests. Breaking all of the tests, that is. With options for understanding the context for the project few and far between, we worked on features in an exploratory way and observed how the existing tests were impacted.

As discussed, tests can help you validate your assumptions, but they also communicate them to future engineers. Even when you don’t quite understand the background for a test, watching tests blow up and then debugging through the setup and acting code helps us learn a lot.

As we built out our new system we ran into massive differences in assumptions between the fields this program now had to reconcile. With other parts of the organization dealing with their own issues while starting their deliveries, we worked on a rolling release basis: we crossed each bridge as we got to it or we built tunnels where we couldn’t fit a bridge. I don’t think Uncle Bob would love a lot of the code the team put out and I know the former developer wouldn’t recognize a lot of the product once we were done with it.

We also added our own new tests, solidifying behaviors and communicating intent. Then we rewrote them, once the intended behavior shifted to match evolving requirements. In fact that also happened to documentation. I made a point of explaining a number of old decisions that had not been obvious to me, as well as my new decisions that may cause future confusion - the results of the mismatched business processes. This documentation evolved as the architecture and implementation evolved - as long as it was under my control, I wished to make the next engineer’s life easier than mine had been. Docs don’t tell the whole story, but they can help.

Ultimately, the previous mental model is lost to the ages. The new one is what’s left after people who never fully understood it crashed a whole separate concept into it and welded and duct-taped them together. I don’t know if I can say it rose from the ashes, but it was certainly on fire.

A Note On The Bus Number Problem

By this point I hope you’ll agree enterprise software is built in and distributed over people’s brains. What happens when the body that holds one of those brains misses a bus and is unavailable? We’ve touched on this with the second case study, but it bears an extra thought.

We’ve discussed how a program dies and is reborn, while pointing out some risks connected to that journey. The obvious approach to prevent these is having a bigger Bus Number - more people need to know about the system, by first-hand experience, thereby limiting how much of the program we lose when we lose one of them. That said, there are practical business limitations to this. Not everybody in a team or business unit can hold all the details of all the projects in their brain.

Ultimately we have to be willing to accept that this process will happen, either gradually or abruptly. We need to trust our people to understand this and deal with it - build in enough time to create some kind of familiarity with the specification and documentation, some time for exploring code, for writing experimentation tests and to talk to users and other stakeholders. We need to put as much of the program as we can into the new team’s brains before we expect them to produce useful code.

Conclusion

I hope to have effectively explained why software systems are created and exist as theories and mental models in people’s minds. Further, this nature of a program means that collaboration is key in development, maintenance, service and use.

In a time when engineers are often expected to produce code they don’t fully understand, it’s vital to realize that code is not the program. Attempting to offload the understanding to another program that will lose its memory and will interpret code and documentation - which may or may not be updated, if it exists at all - non-deterministically is a serious risk. Rejecting opportunities to share experience and thoughts is an even greater one, as the real work, the thoughts, will be lost to an organization, even if the code is stored safely in version control. Time spent guiding a new engineer is an investment in a company asset.

Our tools change and so do our processes, with them, but some problems repeat when we’re not aware of their causes. We’re seeing app launches accelerated by generative AI and then stall their development as the codebase gets out of hand. Uncle Bob explained the drop in velocity in software teams 20 years ago as a failure in architecture, process and standards. Different tools, same problem.

If nothing else, I hope to have left the reader with the eagerness to look at the books I mentioned. I hope they are as useful and inspiring as they’ve been to me.

Happy programming.
